Purpose

Tools like TFS provide large corporations with a seamless way to organize, plan, and execute their software development efforts. Unfortunately, most project management tools focus on individual teams and team workflows, and neglect to include a simple way to aggregate all of a company's development metrics into a single view. PSI Dashboard aims to fill this gap by providing a glanceable frontend that communicates the organization's development progress as a whole.


The PSI Concept

DuPont Pioneer organizes its development cadence into eight week (four sprint) chunks. These chunks are known as Potentially Shippable Increments, or PSIs. At the end of every PSI boundary, each development team is expected to have a releasable product that includes the features that have been under development for the past two months.

While the concept of a regular release schedule is hardly unique to Pioneer, the specific implementation and nomenclature of this process made working with third-party management tools awkward at best.


Interface

To visually present the PSI concept, I chose to place each application's planned features into a zoom-enabled grid, with sequential PSIs forming a timeline along the X axis:

The color of each feature is representative of the development team that has been assigned to that feature. The colored bars on the left side of features and initiatives (Process Management, in this example) represent the overall status of the item. The
small icon in the corner of some features identifies that feature as having a dependency on another.

Hovering over a particular feature brings up a contextual popup with more information about that particular item:

Navigating the Dashboard is much like navigating Google Maps. Clicking and dragging pans the body of the grid through the viewport, and the column and row headers slide to stay in sync. Additionally, the entire view is zoom-enabled through the use of the mouse wheel:
