Rook is one of my oldest and most complex side projects. Built as an homage to the "official" card game of Dordt college, Rook taught me a great deal about how to design software, and more importantly, how not to design software.

The Lobby

The administrative details of grouping players into games, assigning teams, and synchronizing the start of the game was complicated enough to be its very own project. If you find there's no one in the lobby (which is very likely, given this project's popularity), you can always simulate a full team by opening up four tabs and playing against yourself. (Don't know how to play Rook?)


The Cards

To make the game feel as genuine as possible, I took great pains to keep the game board visually appealing. I bought a new deck of Rook cards and scanned every card in batches of four or five. I then rounded the corners and outlined each card with a black border using GIMP. The result was a set of images that look and feel much like a real deck of Rook cards:


Validation

To be sure no one's cheating (i.e., not following suit), every action a player makes is validated server-side before relaying the move out to the other players. However, much of the validation is also enforced on the front-end, through the UI, so it's quite rare that a player can make an invalid move in the first place.
